Lesson 0001 · 8 minutes

The schema is a story, not a pile of SQL.

Your first design skill is not memorizing columns. It is recognizing the job a table performs.

The whole story: StockHawk knows identities, records evidence-backed history, points at the latest facts, and copies those facts into a fast search shape.

Six roles hold the entire schema

1. Identity

storefront, product, retailer_listing

Stable nouns: who or what is this?

2. Intake + proof

observation_batch, source_evidence_artifact

Where did this import and its evidence come from?

3. History

retailer_listing_observation, stock_observation, catalog_match, change_event

What did we know or decide over time?

4. Current pointers

current_listing_state, current_stock_state

Which historical fact counts as “now”?

5. Search copy

search_document and the search_document_source view

What shape makes the UI query easy and fast?

6. Operations

service_heartbeat

Is each backend service alive?

CHANGE_EVENT points back to the batch + observations that caused each important change.

The center of the story is retailer_listing: one product page on one storefront. Most changing facts attach to it. A catalog_match says which canonical product that page represents.

The design rule to steal

Never overwrite history merely to answer “what is true now?” StockHawk stores observations as history, then uses tiny current-state tables as pointers. Search gets a separate denormalized copy.

“Denormalized” means deliberately repeating selected facts in one read-friendly row. The source remains the normalized tables.

Retrieval check

Which role answers “what is stock now?”

What sits at the center of changing retailer facts?

Which role should preserve facts instead of overwriting them?

Your tangible win: before designing any new table, name its role: identity, intake, history, current pointer, read model, or operations.
